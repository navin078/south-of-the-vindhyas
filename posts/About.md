## About

I grew up next to a railway yard on the outskirts of Chennai.

As a child, trains were not something I travelled in. They were simply there: a constant presence beyond the window, announcing themselves with whistles, rumbling past at odd hours, shunting endlessly from one track to another. Some children grow up near the sea. I grew up near steel rails.

The fascination never quite left.

Years later, after retiring from a career in engineering, I found myself with something both precious and dangerous: time. Not a vacation. Not a sabbatical. Time measured in years rather than weeks.

This website is the result.

The idea is simple: travel every railway line in South India, one journey at a time.

There is no schedule. No race. No prize waiting at the finish line.

Some months I may spend only a day on a train. Other months I may disappear for a week. The goal is not efficiency. The goal is attention.

I want to watch the landscape change from coast to plateau to forest to city. I want to eat at station canteens and roadside tiffin shops. I want to listen to conversations I was never meant to hear. I want to visit small stations whose names rarely appear on travel itineraries. I want to see what lies beyond the places through which most people merely pass.

Along the way, I hope to write about trains, food, history, temples, engineering, fellow travellers, and the thousand small observations that make up a journey.

The title, *South of the Vindhyas*, is both a description and a boundary. The rail network south of the Vindhya mountains is vast enough to keep me occupied for years and familiar enough to feel like home.

This is not a guidebook.

It is not an attempt to find the best places, the fastest routes, or the most famous attractions.

It is simply a record of one traveller slowly colouring in a railway map.

If you happen to be on the same train for a while, welcome aboard.

The destination is every station. The journey is everything in between.

## The Map

The grey lines on the map page represent the entire South Indian railway network. As I travel, the lines I've covered will gradually change colour (still deciding which one). Click on a station someday and you might find a story from that place.
For now, the map is mostly empty. That's the point.
